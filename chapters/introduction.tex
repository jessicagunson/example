\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

Animals communicate via complex, multi-component, multi-modal signals that include visual, auditory, olfactory, and tactile components \parencite{fröhlichFunctionPrimateMultimodal2018}. Many colorful and behavioral signals displayed across the animal kingdom evolved as adaptations for female mate choice \parencite{anderssonFemaleChoiceSelects1982,darwinSexualSelectionDescent1871,hillBirdColorationFunction2006}. Mate choice is the central mechanism of intersexual selection, and refers to behavioral and physiological mechanisms displayed by one sex that increases its likelihood of reproducing with members of the opposite sex \parencite{anderssonSexualSelection1994}. Ornaments, traits that evolve as the result of mate choice, are theorized to be condition-dependent, honest signals \parencite{clutton-brockRoaringRedDeer1979,hamiltonHeritableTrueFitness1982,hillPlumageColorationSexually1991,somjeeHiddenCostSexually2018,tibbettsEvolutionHonestCommunication2014}. Ornaments should only evolve and be the subject of assessment if they remain an honest signal of quality, such that only high quality individuals exhibit full signal expression \parencite{heywoodSexualSelectionHandicap1989,somjeeHiddenCostSexually2018,tibbettsEvolutionHonestCommunication2014,weaverWhatMaintainsSignal2017,zahaviMateSelectionSelection1975}. Further, the opposite sex should prefer to mate with these high quality individuals, assuming the mechanism underlying variation in quality is heritable and provides genetic benefits to offspring \parencite{anderssonSexualSelection1994,vonschantzGoodGenesOxidative1999,zahaviMateSelectionSelection1975}. In systems where males exhibit sexual signals and females have some say in who they mate with, it is critical to characterize the different types of information perceived and interpreted by receivers to decipher if and how that information is used in reproductive decision-making \parencite{highamIntroductionMultimodalCommunication2013}. While the role of male sexual signals and female mate choice in mammals has been largely overshadowed by mechanisms of direct male-male competition, they remain an integral piece to the larger picture of sexual selection.
